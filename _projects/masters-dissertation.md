# Master's Dissertation

From Orientalist philologists to colonial historians, many European encounters in the nineteenth and early-twentieth centuries tended to conflate Indian philosophy with religion, mythology, and mysticism. Indian intellectual traditions were variously romanticized as representations of a pristine spiritual antiquity or dismissed and vilified as “monstrous superstitions.”<sup><a href="#fn1" id="ref1">[1]</a></sup>

Later scholars in the late-twentieth century explicitly countered this charge – often “‘leaning over backwards" to show the analytic nature of Indian philosophy.<sup><a href="#fn2" id="ref2">[2]</a></sup>

More recent approaches continue to navigate this tension in different ways, but what unites them is that they lead to concrete decisions about what counts as legitimate philosophical evidence and how it should be handled — whether through a focus on conceptual clarity and textual analysis (the analytic approach), or through a broader engagement with practice, materiality, and lived experience (the spiritual, mythological, religious approach).

Rather than treating these approaches as competing, I take them as different ways of making certain aspects of Indian philosophy visible. My dissertation adopts the claim implicit in both: that Indian philosophy can be studied not only as a set of abstract logic/arguments, but as a way of organizing personal/lived experience. 

The dissertation focuses on the Sringeri Maṭha in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries as a site in which philosophical, ritual, institutional, and spatial elements intersect. I treat its central philosophy, Advaita Vedānta, as a “worldview”—a historically located pattern of reasoning, practice, and meaning. Texts such as the Panchadasi provide an entry point into a “common-sense” Advaita worldview, while additional works, inscriptions, architecture, and institutional sources help reconstruct the world that produced, maintained, and authorized that worldview. Taken together, this approach treats Advaita Vedānta as both a worldview and a world that produced this view, allowing it to be understood not as a static set of philosophical claims, but as a historically embedded form of life. 

The project aims to show that classical Indian philosophy perhaps becomes most intelligible not when abstracted from religion, nor when collapsed into it, but when treated as a historically embedded form of lived experience.
